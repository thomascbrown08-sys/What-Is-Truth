Session Six

Truth on Its Feet

“So I’ve studied. I’ve sought. I’ve asked Him to open my eyes. Now what—is knowing the point?”

It is the right question to end on, because it exposes a trap we could have walked into over these five sessions: treating truth as something to collect. We can become experts in doctrine, keepers of correct opinions, and stay—in the only way that matters—untouched. The Bible won’t allow it. In Scripture, truth is never just something the mind owns. It is a road the whole person walks. It was always meant to get up off the page and onto its feet.

Jesus said as much to a group who had just started to believe Him. And notice that He points right past believing, to abiding, and past abiding, to freedom:

If you abide in my word, you are truly my disciples, and you will know the truth, and the truth will set you free.

John 8:31–32

That last line is one of the most quoted—and most chopped-up—sentences in the world. “The truth will set you free” gets carved over library doors as if it meant that information sets us loose, that education alone breaks every chain. But read what Jesus actually ties it to. The truth that frees is not free-floating facts. It is His word, abided in. And the freedom He means is not vague self-improvement. When His hearers push back, He names the slavery exactly: “everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin” (John 8:34). The chains are moral and spiritual, and we cannot file them off by knowing more. Which is why He finishes: “If the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed” (John 8:36). The truth that frees is, in the end, a Person who does the freeing. We don’t free ourselves with our study. We are freed by the One we have been studying.

A road, not a trophyWalking in the truth

The aged apostle John, near the end of his life, writes to a friend and lets us overhear what makes him happiest. It is not that his readers have mastered the truth. It is that they are walking in it:

For I rejoiced greatly when the brothers came and testified to your truth, as indeed you are walking in the truth. I have no greater joy than to hear that my children are walking in the truth.

3 John 3–4

“Walking”—such an ordinary word. Not soaring in the truth. Not lecturing in the truth. Not winning arguments in the truth. Walking. One plain step after another, in the direction the truth points, on an unremarkable Tuesday. That is what truth looks like once it has feet: a life slowly shaped to fit what it says it believes. And it is the test the New Testament comes back to again and again. “Be doers of the word, and not hearers only, deceiving yourselves” (James 1:22). “Let us not love in word or talk but in deed and in truth” (1 John 3:18). The house that stands in the storm, Jesus said, is built by the one who hears His words “and does them” (Matthew 7:24).

Truth you will not live, you do not yet believe.

That is a hard saying, so let’s handle it with care—because read wrongly it tips straight into the legalism this study has worked hard to avoid. It does not mean your standing before God hangs on your performance, as if you had to live perfectly to prove your faith is real. It means something more searching. Real belief is not just agreeing in your head, and it always, sooner or later, moves the feet. A “faith” that changes nothing was never the trusting kind. It was the demons’ kind—they too believe the facts, and shudder, and stay exactly as they are (James 2:19). Real faith leans its weight on Christ, and a person leaning their weight somewhere gets moved. Bonhoeffer pressed the two together until they were one motion:

Only he who believes is obedient, and only he who is obedient believes.

Dietrich Bonhoeffer · Discipleship

The obedience is not the price of the believing. It is the pulse of it—the sign that the heart is alive. And it is not something we crank out on our own. The same Spirit who opened our eyes in Session Five is the one who now moves our feet. We don’t walk in the truth by gritting our teeth. We walk because the truth has taken hold of us and the Spirit is at work inside. The doing flows out of the gift. It does not buy it.

In Plain Language

Two old words name the two halves of a whole Christian. Orthodoxy means “right belief”—getting the truth right. Orthopraxy means “right practice”—living it out. This study has spent most of its time on the first, because you cannot live what you don’t know. But the first was always for the second. Right belief that never reaches the hands and feet has stalled halfway. Right practice with no roots in truth is a cut flower—pretty for a day, then dead. God means to grow the whole plant.

The wound, healedTruth carried in love

Now we can come back to the ache that opened Session Three—the reader who was handed “the truth” as a weapon. Here is Scripture’s answer, and it is not what wounded people often fear it will be. The answer to truth-used-as-a-club is not less truth. It is truth finally carried the way God always meant it to be carried—in love:

Rather, speaking the truth in love, we are to grow up in every way into him who is the head, into Christ.

Ephesians 4:15

Paul won’t let us split them. Truth without love becomes a club—correct and cruel, the gatekeeper’s tool. But love without truth is not love at all. It is just being nice—too cowardly or too indifferent to tell you what you most need to hear. Real love is committed to the other person’s good, so in the end it cannot lie to them. The goal of the two together is growth—“we are to grow up… into Christ.” So if truth was once swung at you without love, you did not meet too much truth. You met truth ripped out of the only hands fit to hold it. The cure is to see it held rightly at last—by the One who is Himself full of grace and truth, and who never once used it to crush a bruised reed.

Deeper Waters

On the night before He died, Jesus prayed for His followers: “Sanctify them in the truth; your word is truth” (John 17:17). Sanctify means to make holy—and notice the tool: God makes us holy by means of the truth. This is why studying Scripture is never just gathering information. It is one of the main ways the Spirit reshapes us. Paul describes how it works as a kind of looking: “we all, with unveiled face, beholding the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image” (2 Corinthians 3:18). We become like what we gaze at. Fix your eyes on a true and glorious God, and over time—slowly, often without noticing—the soul is shaped to match what it beholds. The change is God’s work (“this comes from the Lord who is the Spirit”), not ours. Our part is to keep looking. There is a whole doctrine of how God makes us holy in that one verse—and a lifetime of looking ahead.

The sendingTruth was never meant to be hoarded

There is one last place for truth to go, and it is outward. The God who found you did not find you so you could become a private collector of correct beliefs. The seeker becomes a believer. The believer becomes one who is sent. Jesus’ final words to His disciples turn them around to face the world:

Go therefore and make disciples of all nations… teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age.

Matthew 28:19–20

But carry it the way this whole study has insisted. Not the way the gatekeeper carried it—not as a weapon, not as a test others must pass to earn your acceptance, not from above. Carry it as one beggar telling another where bread was found. The world will not finally be won by how sharp our arguments are, true as they may be. Jesus named the evidence the world would actually read: “By this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another” (John 13:35). Truth on its feet walks toward people, in love, with good news.

The end, and the beginningStay in the room

We began with a man who asked the right question and walked out before the answer. Pilate stood a few feet from the Truth and turned away. Over six sessions, you have done the opposite. You have stayed. You asked what truth is and found it was finally a Who. You saw that you cannot love a God you will not know, and that no sincere idol can stand in for the living One. You learned what an honest mind looks like, and that the God of truth is gentle with the wounded and hard on the proud. You were handed tools for reading, and shown that the reading runs toward a Person. You watched the old fight between thinking and trusting end in faith seeking understanding—and learned that even your seeing is a gift. And now you’ve seen that all of it was meant to get up and walk.

So this ending is really a sending. Go and walk in the truth—abiding in His word, leaning your weight on the Son who sets you free, speaking the truth in love, looking at His glory until you are slowly changed into it, and carrying the good news out to others still standing in Pilate’s court. The studying is not over. It has only found its purpose. And you do not go alone: “Behold, I am with you always.”

Stay in the room. Then go, and bring others in.

Reflection & Discussiontap to open ›

For personal reflection or small-group conversation. This is the last session—let it move from the head to the hands.

  1. We warned against treating truth as something to collect. Be honest: where are you tempted to be an expert in correct beliefs while staying unchanged by them?
  2. Jesus ties freedom to abiding in His word, and finally to the Son Himself setting us free. How is that different from the popular idea that “the truth” (information, education) sets us free on its own?
  3. “Truth you will not live, you do not yet believe.” We were careful to say this isn’t about earning your standing. How would you explain the difference between “obedience is the pulse of faith” and “obedience is the price of faith”?
  4. The answer to weaponized truth is truth carried in love (Ephesians 4:15)—not less truth. Does that change anything about a painful experience you or someone you know has had with the church?
  5. 2 Corinthians 3:18 says we’re changed by looking—we become like what we gaze at. Practically, what are you looking at most these days? What is it shaping you into?
  6. The study ends by turning you outward. Who is still “in Pilate’s court” in your life—asking the question but not yet staying for the answer? What would it look like to walk toward them in truth and love this week?
