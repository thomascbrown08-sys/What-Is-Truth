Session One

What Is Truth?

The most famous question ever asked about truth was asked by a man standing a few feet from the answer. Then he walked out.

It is the trial of Jesus, and Pilate is losing control of the room. He has a prisoner who will not play along. So he asks the question every age comes back to: “What is truth?” John writes it down in a single, heavy line.

Pilate said to him, “What is truth?” After he had said this, he went back outside to the Jews and told them, “I find no guilt in him.”

John 18:38

Notice what John does not write down: an answer. Pilate asks, and then turns away. He does not wait. And here is the terrible irony. The answer was a Person standing right in front of him—the one who had said, a few chapters earlier, “I am the way, and the truth, and the life” (John 14:6). Pilate had truth in handcuffs, and he asked where to find it.

It is tempting to feel better than Pilate. We shouldn’t. His question is a posture as much as a sentence. It is the shrug of a man who has already decided the question has no answer worth waiting for—or that the answer will cost more than he wants to pay. That shrug is still around. It just wears modern clothes: “That’s true for you.” “Who’s to say?” “Everyone has their own truth.” Sometimes it is honest confusion. Sometimes, as we’ll see later, it is a way to leave the room before you have to hear the answer.

So before we go anywhere else, let’s do what Pilate would not. Let’s stay, and ask the question slowly. What is truth?

The shapes of the questionTruth comes in more than one shape

Part of why this question ties us in knots is that the word “truth” does more than one job. It helps a lot to pull those jobs apart.

The most basic kind is what thinkers call truth as correspondence. A statement is true when it matches the way things really are. “It is raining” is true only if it is, in fact, raining. Truth here is not a feeling or a vote. It is a match between what we say and what is real. The rain does not care whether you believe in it. You still get wet. This is what we mean by “true” most of the time, and it is worth saying out loud, because our age is oddly embarrassed by it: reality has a shape, and it is not the shape of our wishes.

In Plain Language

Correspondence just means “matching up.” A true statement matches reality the way a key matches a lock, or a map matches the land. If the map shows a bridge and you walk off a cliff, the problem isn’t that you weren’t sincere. The problem is that the map didn’t match the ground.

One more word will show up later: epistemology (ih-pis-teh-MOL-uh-jee). It just means “the study of how we know things”—how we tell what’s real from what only looks real. You don’t need the word to do the thinking. But it’s good to know there’s a name for the country we’re walking into.

There is a second kind of truth that we almost forget today, and the Bible cares about it deeply. It is the difference between knowing that and knowing whom. I can know that a thousand facts about a person are correct—their height, their hometown, their job history—and still not know the person at all. Knowing a person goes deeper than holding correct facts about them. It takes trust, time, meeting, and faithfulness proven over the years. The Hebrew Scriptures use one verb, yada‘, for both kinds of knowing—knowing facts and knowing people—because to the biblical mind they belong together. The deepest truth is not just correct. It is known, the way a face is known.

And there is a third shape: truth as faithfulness. In the Bible, a thing is “true” when you can lean on it—when it holds. A true friend is not one whose existence can be proven, but one who shows up. When the Bible calls God “true,” it means at least this: He will not turn out to be different from how He has shown Himself to be. He keeps faith. You can put your weight on Him, and He holds.

Hold those three together—truth that matches reality, truth that is personally known, truth that proves faithful—and something surprising happens. They line up. They all point past themselves to one source.

Deeper Waters

Thinkers have offered several “theories of truth.” The correspondence theory (truth matches reality) is the oldest, and most Christians would call it the bedrock one. Others include the coherence theory (a belief is true if it fits without contradiction into a bigger web of beliefs) and the pragmatic theory (a belief is “true” if it works, if acting on it succeeds).

Each one catches something real. Coherence is why a good detective distrusts a story that doesn’t hang together; the truth usually does fit. Workability is why we trust a medicine that heals. But watch the danger if you make either one the whole story. A lie can be perfectly consistent. A comfortable falsehood can “work” for a while. That is the fatal flaw in “whatever works for you”: reality eventually sends the bill. This is a whole country of thought, and you don’t have to cross all of it. But it’s worth knowing it’s there—and worth knowing that the Christian instinct anchors truth not in our agreement or our success, but in something solid underneath both.

The turnNot first a what, but a Who

Here is the move that changes everything. It is the reason this whole study exists. In the Bible, truth is not finally some property that statements happen to have. Truth is rooted in the character of a Person. Before truth is a quality of sentences, it is a quality of God.

That is why Jesus does not say, “I will teach you the truth,” or even “I will tell you the truth,” though both are true. He says, “I am the truth.” He is claiming that reality, at its deepest level, is not a cold machine but a faithful Person. To line up with what is most real is to be rightly related to Him. The matching kind of truth, the personal kind, and the faithful kind all come home to the same place.

Truth is not a thing God knows. It is a Person God is.

This is the old confidence of the church. Sixteen centuries ago, Augustine was wrestling with whether a Christian could learn anything true from non-Christian writers. He decided yes, gladly—because all truth has one owner:

Let every good and true Christian understand that wherever truth may be found, it belongs to his Master.

Augustine of Hippo · On Christian Doctrine, II.18

That one sentence does two things at once, and we’ll lean on both for the rest of the study. First, it means no truth anywhere is a rival to God—not in science, not in history, not in the hard thing a critic says about us. If it is true, it is His, and we can follow it without fear. A Christian never has to be afraid of an honest fact. Second, it means truth is not something we make up. It is something we find, because it was His before it was ours.

This is why the Bible can call God Himself by the name of faithfulness—He is “Faithful and True” (Revelation 19:11)—and why John can say the Word who became flesh was “full of grace and truth” (John 1:14). Truth is not a cold rule hanging over the universe that even God must obey. Truth is the reliability of God’s own character, shining out into everything He has made. Reality can be trusted because He can be trusted.

Where knowing beginsThe fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge

If truth is rooted in God, then there is a startling result for anyone who wants to find it. The first step in knowing anything truly is not a technique. It is a posture toward the One who owns all of it.

The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge; fools despise wisdom and instruction.

Proverbs 1:7

“Fear” here is not terror. It is the awe of a creature who has finally remembered that he did not make himself, or the world. It is the opposite of Pilate’s shrug. Pilate’s question failed not because he wasn’t smart—he was an educated Roman. It failed because he had no awe. He came to the biggest question of his life as a man who had already decided it didn’t matter. The text says he “went back outside” before the answer could land. There is the whole tragedy in two words.

Calvin opened his great book by tying two kinds of knowing together—knowing God and knowing ourselves—and saying you can’t have one without the other:

Our wisdom… consists almost entirely of two parts: the knowledge of God and of ourselves.

John Calvin · Institutes of the Christian Religion, I.1.1

That is the road this study walks. To seek truth is, in the end, to seek God. And in seeking Him, we finally learn the truth about ourselves too. The seeker who came here wanting to know whether any of this is real is asking exactly the right question. The believer who wants to go deeper is being invited into the two knowings Calvin named. And the one who was wounded by people who used “the truth” as a weapon—take heart. The truth we are after is not a club. It is a Person who was Himself falsely accused, who stood in Pilate’s court and bore the lie, and who is called Faithful and True because He keeps faith with those who seek Him.

Pilate asked the right question and walked out before the answer. We are going to stay in the room.

Reflection & Discussiontap to open ›

For personal reflection or small-group conversation. There are no trick questions here—only honest ones. Let the answers be real.

  1. Pilate asks “What is truth?” and walks out before the answer. Where have you seen that move—asking a question you didn’t really want answered—in the world around you? Where have you seen it in yourself?
  2. We named three shapes of truth: matching reality, knowing a person, and proving faithful. Which one do you think of first when you hear the word “truth”? Which is easiest to forget?
  3. What would change this week if you really believed that every true thing belongs to God—so that no honest fact could ever be a threat to your faith?
  4. Jesus says “I am the truth,” not “I will tell you the truth.” What changes when truth is a Person to be known, not just a fact to be filed away?
  5. “The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge.” What does it look like to start a search for truth in awe instead of a shrug? Try to be specific.
