Session Five

Faith Seeking Understanding

Two voices each claim to be the only honest option. One says: prove it, and then I’ll believe. The other says: stop thinking, and just have faith. Scripture turns down both.

You have heard both voices, maybe in the same week. The first is the voice of the skeptic—but also of a certain kind of believer. It says faith is irresponsible until reason signs off: show me the evidence, satisfy my mind, and then I’ll commit. The second is the voice of a tired piety. It says thinking is the enemy of trust: the holiest thing you can do with a hard question is stop asking it; just believe. They sound like opposites. They’re usually handed to us as the only two choices. And here is the strange thing this session will unfold. Each one is protecting something true—and each one, left alone, becomes a way of not meeting God at all.

The old fightTwo half-truths at each other’s throats

Let’s give the two voices their proper names. And here is the key move: let’s find the good instinct under each one before we say where it goes wrong. Neither side is simply foolish. Both are guarding something the other forgets.

Two errors, each guarding something real

Rationalism

“I’ll believe what I can prove. Faith has to wait for reason’s verdict.”

What it gets right: that truth matters, that faith should not be blind or gullible, that God gave us minds and is not honored when we switch them off. Christianity makes claims—Jesus rose, or He didn’t—and claims can be examined. This instinct is right to take that seriously.

Where it overreaches: it quietly crowns the human mind as the final judge that even God must satisfy. But Session Three already broke that throne. There is no neutral judge. The mind doing the “objective” weighing is the same mind that suppresses the truth it fears. Rationalism asks God to prove Himself to a juror who has a reason to let himself off the hook.

Fideism

“Don’t think—just believe. Reason is faith’s rival.” (FID-ay-ism, from the Latin for “faith”)

What it gets right: that God is bigger than our logic, that no one is ever argued into the kingdom by brainpower alone, that faith is finally trust in a Person and not the end of a proof. You cannot calculate your way into love. This instinct is right that something more than reasoning is needed.

Where it overreaches: it sets faith against the mind God told us to love Him with (Session Two), and turns belief into a leap into the dark—empty, untestable, no different from wishful thinking. A faith that refuses every question can’t tell the living God from a comforting idol. It guards trust by cutting off truth.

The Resolution

The mistake in both is the order they assume—that you must finish one before you can start the other. Rationalism says understand first, then maybe believe. Fideism says believe instead of understanding. The ancient church found a third way that ends the fight: faith seeking understanding. We believe in order to understand. Trust comes first—not as a blind leap, but as the reasonable response to a Person who has proven trustworthy—and that trust then sets the mind free to explore everything.

This third way has a name that is a thousand years old: fides quaerens intellectum, “faith seeking understanding.” Anselm coined the phrase, and he stated the order plainly:

I do not seek to understand in order that I may believe, but I believe in order that I may understand. For… unless I believed, I should not understand.

Anselm of Canterbury · Proslogion, ch. 1

He was building on Augustine, who taught the same order—crede ut intelligas, “believe so that you may understand”—reading it out of the prophet’s line, “Unless you believe, you will not understand” (Isaiah 7:9, in the form Augustine knew it). This is not anti-thinking. It is the opposite. Anselm and Augustine were among the sharpest minds the church has ever produced. They are saying that faith is not the end of thinking but its beginning—the door you step through, after which everything becomes thinkable. Trust the Person first. Then bring Him your hardest questions, and watch them open.

Faith is not the opposite of thinking; it is thinking that has finally found its feet.

Why must trust come first? Because some things can only be known from the inside. You don’t check out a friend’s faithfulness from a safe distance and then risk the friendship. You trust, and in trusting you come to know. You don’t prove a chair will hold you by study alone. At some point you sit down. Faith is the sitting down. And it is not a leap into the dark—it is a step into the light by which everything else becomes visible. Lewis said it unforgettably, at the close of an essay arguing that Christianity is not just a beautiful story but a true one:

I believe in Christianity as I believe that the Sun has risen, not only because I see it, but because by it I see everything else.

C. S. Lewis · “Is Theology Poetry?”

That answers both voices at once. To the rationalist: you are asking to see the sun before you’ll admit there’s light to see by—but it is by the sun that you see at all. To the fideist: this is no blind leap. It is the most clear-eyed thing imaginable, because by this light “I see everything else.” Faith is the place you stand to see, not the blindfold over your eyes.

The problem under the problemWe cannot open our own eyes

But now we hit the deepest difficulty, and it would sink us if the session ended here. If faith comes first—if we must believe in order to understand—then where does the faith come from? Session Three told us the lens is smudged from the inside. We hold down the truth we fear. So we cannot just decide to see clearly, any more than a blind man can decide to see. The very trust that everything depends on is the one thing our dark hearts will not produce on their own. Paul states the bind without flinching:

The natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are folly to him, and he is not able to understand them because they are spiritually discerned.

1 Corinthians 2:14

Not able. Not just unwilling—unable, on his own. The gospel is not a puzzle the clever will solve and the simple will miss. It is “folly” to the natural mind across the board—“the word of the cross is folly to those who are perishing” (1 Corinthians 1:18). If anyone is ever going to really see, Someone outside the smudged lens has to open the eyes. And that is exactly what God does.

The Spirit who leads into truthIllumination

The same passage that names our inability turns right around to the rescue. What we could never reach, God reveals:

These things God has revealed to us through the Spirit. For the Spirit searches everything, even the depths of God… Now we have received… the Spirit who is from God, that we might understand the things freely given us by God.

1 Corinthians 2:10, 12

This is the work theologians call illumination: the Holy Spirit opening blind eyes to recognize, receive, and rest in the truth He Himself wrote. Jesus promised exactly this—“When the Spirit of truth comes, he will guide you into all the truth” (John 16:13). The honest reader’s prayer from the last session—“open my eyes”—is answered not by our effort, but by His.

In Plain Language

Two words that sound alike and are not. Revelation is God giving the truth—chiefly in Scripture, finished and complete. Illumination is God’s Spirit turning on the light so we can see the truth that’s already there. Illumination doesn’t add new messages, and it doesn’t skip past the Bible. It opens our eyes to the Bible. So “the Spirit told me” is never a pass to go around Scripture—the same Spirit breathed out the Scripture and will not contradict Himself. He works through the Word, not around it.

This guards us from two dangers at once. It saves us from cold rationalism, because no amount of study can replace the Spirit’s light—you can master the text and never see the glory. And it saves us from runaway mysticism, because the Spirit’s leading is never a private hotline that overrides what God has plainly said. He lights up the Word; He does not replace it. Calvin put our deepest certainty here—not finally in outside arguments, good as they are, but in the Spirit’s inner witness, confirming God’s own Word to us:

The testimony of the Spirit is more excellent than all reason.

John Calvin · Institutes, I.7.4

Deeper Waters

Two modern thinkers carried this forward, and they’re worth knowing about. Cornelius Van Til pushed the point of Session Three all the way: there is no neutral ground from which to judge God, because every chain of reasoning rests on starting assumptions—and the unbeliever’s assumptions already rule out the God he claims to be weighing. We never reason from nowhere. Alvin Plantinga argued that belief in God can be “properly basic”—reasonable without being built on earlier premises, much like your belief that the world is more than five minutes old—and that if God exists and made us to know Him, then a mind whose eyes He opens is not malfunctioning but finally working as designed. You could spend years in these waters. The one pearl to carry away: the demand that faith justify itself before a “neutral” reason was never a fair fight, because the neutral ground does not exist—and the God who made our minds is the One best able to heal them.

The whole fight at restWe study because we already trust

Now everything in this study can sit in one hand. The rationalist was right that the mind matters—so we study, carefully, with all five habits of Session Four. The fideist was right that trust comes first and reaches what reason alone cannot—so we believe, and lean our weight on the Person, not just the proofs. And the deepest truth swallows them both: we can do either one only because God, by His Spirit, has opened our eyes. Even the faith is His gift—“by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God” (Ephesians 2:8–9). Even the studying is grace at work in us—“work out your own salvation… for it is God who works in you” (Philippians 2:12–13).

This is meant to take pressure off, not pile it on. You are not trying to force faith by sheer mental effort, like someone straining to believe harder. You are not the source of your own sight. You are a person whose eyes are being opened—and your study, your seeking, your honest questions are the very tools the Spirit loves to use to open them further. So you can dig with everything you’ve got and rest at the same time. The work is real and the rest is real, because both are His work in you.

We study because we already trust—and even the studying is grace.

So pray as you read. Not as a religious formality, but because the One who wrote the book is the only One who can open your eyes to it—and He loves to be asked. “Open my eyes, that I may behold wondrous things” is not a beggar’s long shot. It is a child asking a Father for the very thing the Father most wants to give.

Reflection & Discussiontap to open ›

For personal reflection or small-group conversation. This is the heart of the study—don’t rush it.

  1. Which of the two voices—“prove it first” or “just believe, stop thinking”—have you heard more often? Which one lives more comfortably in you?
  2. We tried to name the real thing each error is guarding. What is rationalism right to protect? What is fideism right to protect? Why does naming the good instinct matter before pointing out the error?
  3. Anselm: “I believe in order that I may understand.” Where in your own life did you only understand something after you trusted—a relationship, a calling, a hard teaching?
  4. Lewis compares faith to the sun: we believe “not only because I see it, but because by it I see everything else.” What does Christ help you see that you couldn’t see without Him?
  5. Illumination means the Spirit opens our eyes to truth that’s already in the Word—He doesn’t skip past Scripture. Why is that an important guardrail? Where have you seen “the Spirit told me” used to go around the Bible?
  6. “We study because we already trust—and even the studying is grace.” Does that take pressure off you, or is it hard to believe your seeking is itself a gift? Talk honestly about why.
