Session Three

The Honest Mind

“Hasn’t ‘truth’ always been a weapon? Every person who ever hurt me was absolutely sure they had it.”

If that is your question, this session was written with you in mind. It is not a trap. Before anything else, let’s say it plainly: people have done real damage in the name of “the truth.” They have used it to control, to shame, to shut down honest questions, to stay on top. If that was done to you, your flinch is not a spiritual failure. It is a wound. And the Bible, far from defending the people who did it, has harder words for them than you might expect.

So let’s draw a line that the rest of this session will keep drawing. It is not a line between doubters and the certain. It is a line between two completely different ways of relating to truth. There is the person who seeks the truth, willing to be changed by it. And there is the person who uses the truth, swinging it around while never bowing to it. The second person can be very confident and very wrong. The first can be full of doubt and very near the kingdom. We are after the first kind of mind. Call it the honest mind.

The uncomfortable twistWe are not neutral

Here is the thing almost everyone assumes and the Bible quietly denies. We assume the human mind is a neutral tool, a clean lens—that if we just gather enough evidence, the truth will show up on its own. We picture ourselves as fair judges weighing the case. The Bible says we are nothing of the kind. We come to the question of God with our thumb already on the scale—and the scale tips away.

Paul makes the charge with great precision. The problem is not that the truth about God is missing. The problem is what we do with it:

For the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and unrighteousness of men, who by their unrighteousness suppress the truth. For what can be known about God is plain to them, because God has shown it to them… So they are without excuse. For although they knew God, they did not honor him as God or give thanks to him, but they became futile in their thinking, and their foolish hearts were darkened.

Romans 1:18–21

Read what Paul does not say. He does not say the truth is hidden and we’re innocently in the dark. He says the truth is “plain,” and we suppress it—we hold it down, the way you hold a beach ball under water. That takes effort. It takes constant pressure. And the verse shows the cost: holding the truth down does not leave the mind unharmed. “They became futile in their thinking.” The heart that refuses the light grows dark. Sin doesn’t just make us guilty. It makes us unreliable witnesses about God. The lens is smudged from the inside.

In Plain Language

Theologians call this the noetic effects of sin (NO-eh-tik—from a Greek word for “mind”). It means sin damaged not just our behavior but our thinking, especially our thinking about God. We are not blank, fair-minded jurors. We are jurors with a motive—and the motive bends what we’re willing to see.

This is not an insult to your intelligence. Brilliant people suppress the truth brilliantly. It’s a statement about the will: there are things we don’t see because, deep down, we’d rather not.

Pascal saw this clearly three and a half centuries ago, and he refused to let unbelief pretend it was purely about reason:

Men despise religion; they hate it and fear it is true.

Blaise Pascal · Pensées, 187

That is a stunning line. Not “they doubt it is true”—they fear it is true. Because if it is true, everything has to change. The control has to go. The accounts have to be settled. The throne of one’s own life has to be handed over. A lot of what looks like cool, evidence-based rejection of God is, underneath, a refusal with a motive—not the verdict of a free mind, but the verdict of a mind protecting something. We are very good at calling our preferences “conclusions.”

The reversalWho, exactly, is guilty?

Now slow down, because this is where the wounded need to pay attention. If the deep problem is suppressing the truth—holding it down to protect ourselves—then think about who is actually doing that.

The honest doubter who says, “I’m not sure this is true, and I’m wrestling with it, and I want to know” is not suppressing the truth. That person is seeking it. The very ache of the doubt is a kind of search. But the gatekeeper who used “the truth” as a leash—who shut down questions, punished honesty, and demanded a confidence he was using to stay in control—that person may be suppressing the truth most of all. He never bowed to it. He only swung it around. He kept the right name on the wrong thing, just like at Sinai. Religious certainty can itself be a clever way of refusing God—dressing self-protection up in the language of devotion.

So the Bible’s sharpest words here are not aimed where the wounded fear they are aimed. Jesus was always gentle with the doubting, the questioning, the “help my unbelief” crowd. The people He thundered at were the certain ones—the religious gatekeepers who used their grip on “truth” to pile burdens on others and shut the kingdom in people’s faces. If “truth” was swung at you like a weapon, the One who is the Truth is on record against exactly that.

Doubt that seeks is on its way home. Doubt that hides has already chosen.

This is the difference that sorts everything out. There is a doubt that is honest—restless, searching, willing to be answered, even hoping to be. And there is a “doubt” that is really a decision in disguise—a refusal to look, dressed up as open-mindedness, that wouldn’t believe no matter what it found. The first is the doorway to faith. Tim Keller once said that a faith with no doubts is like a body with no antibodies—untested, and dangerously weak when illness comes. Honest doubt is not the enemy of faith. It is often faith’s growing pains.

Deeper Waters

Calvin argued that every human being carries a sensus divinitatis—a “sense of the divine,” a built-in awareness that there is a God we answer to. We don’t start from zero and reason our way to God. We start already knowing more than we admit, and we spend energy talking ourselves out of it. The modern philosopher Alvin Plantinga built this into a careful case that belief in God can be completely rational without being the end of an argument—much like your belief that other people have minds, or that the past really happened, is rational without proof. You don’t have to follow all of that to feel the point. The demand that God prove Himself to a “neutral” mind may be rigged from the start—because there is no neutral mind, and maybe no neutral place to stand and judge the Maker of the standing-place. There is much more water here. For now, it’s enough to know the shore is not where we assumed.

What honesty looks likeThe mind willing to be wrong

If we are not neutral, then honesty is not having no bias. It is being willing to have our bias exposed. The honest mind is not the one with no motives. It is the one that says, “Show me where I’m protecting myself, and I’ll let go.” That is a posture you can actually take today, whatever your level of certainty.

The Bible even praises a group for it. When Paul preached in Berea, the people did not swallow his message because he was impressive, nor reject it because it was new. They tested it:

Now these Jews were more noble than those in Thessalonica; they received the word with all eagerness, examining the Scriptures daily to see if these things were so.

Acts 17:11

Notice the beautiful balance: “all eagerness” and “examining… to see if these things were so.” Open-hearted and clear-eyed at once. They were neither cynics who couldn’t receive nor pushovers who couldn’t test. And Luke calls this noble. The God of the Bible is not threatened by your fact-checking. He invites it. A faith that forbids examination is not protecting God. It is hiding something.

But there is one more move the honest mind has to make, and it is the deepest, because it goes after the problem at its root. If my own thinking is bent away from God by a will that prefers the dark, then I can’t simply trust my own check on myself. The smudge is on the inside of the lens. I can’t wipe it clean by looking harder through it. So the honest mind finally turns and prays the most dangerous prayer in the Psalms:

Search me, O God, and know my heart! Try me and know my thoughts! And see if there be any grievous way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting!

Psalm 139:23–24

That is the honest mind’s native language. Not “I’ve got it figured out,” but “Search me—I can’t fully see myself, so You look.” It admits that the cure for a self-fooling heart is not more willpower but a God who can see what we hide from ourselves, and who is gentle enough to show us slowly. We’ll come back in Session Five to how He does that work in us. For now it is enough to ask Him to start.

A word to the woundedThe God of truth is not your accuser

If you came to this session braced for another lecture from someone certain, let this be the last thing you read here. The Bible’s portrait of God is not the gatekeeper who hurt you. It is the One who stood in Pilate’s court—falsely accused, on the receiving end of weaponized “truth”—and did not break. He describes Himself like this: “a bruised reed he will not break, and a faintly burning wick he will not quench” (Isaiah 42:3). If your faith is a reed already bent, He is not coming to snap it. If it is a wick barely glowing, He is not coming to pinch it out. The honest, wounded, half-believing seeker is exactly the person He is gentlest with. “Come to me,” He says, “…for I am gentle and lowly in heart” (Matthew 11:28–29).

You don’t have to manufacture certainty to come. You only have to be honest, and turn toward the light instead of away from it. That turning—however small, however shaky—is the most truthful thing a human being can do.

Reflection & Discussiontap to open ›

For personal reflection or small-group conversation. This session touches old wounds for some. Let the room be safe; let no one be rushed.

  1. We told apart seeking the truth from using it. Have you met both kinds of people in church settings? What did each one feel like to be around?
  2. The “noetic effects of sin” means our thinking about God is bent by what we’d rather be true. Can you name a place where your preferences might be quietly shaping your conclusions—in either direction, toward belief or away from it?
  3. Pascal says people often fear Christianity is true, not just doubt it. Is that ever true of you, or of someone you love? What would be costly if it turned out to be true?
  4. We said honest doubt can be the doorway to faith, while confident certainty can be a way of hiding. Does that change how you see your own doubts—or someone whose certainty once wounded you?
  5. Psalm 139:23–24 asks God to search us because we can’t fully see ourselves. What makes that a scary prayer? What makes it a safe one, given who God has shown Himself to be?
