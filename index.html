A Study in Seeking Truth

What Is Truth?

Learning to study, to seek, and to know the God who is really there — and why no real relationship with Him can rest on anything less.

The most famous question ever asked about truth came from a man who walked away before he heard the answer. He was standing a few feet from it at the time. This study is for anyone who would rather stay in the room.

It is written for three kinds of people. You may be more than one of them. There is the seeker, who wants to know whether any of this is real before betting a life on it. There is the believer, who already trusts Christ but senses there is more, and wants to learn how to dig. And there is the person wounded by the church—handed “the truth” as a weapon, with good reason to flinch at the word. There is room here for all of you. In fact, the honest doubter and the wounded skeptic are safer in these pages than the person who is sure of everything and curious about nothing.

We do not study our way to God. We study because God has already found us — and the finding makes us want to know Him.

Over six sessions we will ask some plain, huge questions. What is truth? Why is knowing it not optional, but part of loving God? What makes a mind honest? How do you actually read the Bible? How do faith and thinking fit together instead of fighting? And how is truth meant to be lived, not just held? Each session is a 10–15 minute read with reflection questions you can open at the end.

I

What Is Truth?

Pilate’s question, asked to Truth Himself. The different things we mean by “truth,” and why it turns out to be not first a what but a Who.

“Truth is not a thing God knows. It is a Person God is.”
II

You Cannot Love What You Will Not Know

The heart of the matter—and the demand. Why a sincerely worshiped false picture of God is not a relationship with God at all.

“You cannot love a God you are unwilling to know.”
III

The Honest Mind

We are not neutral judges. The difference between honest seeking and hiding from the truth—and why the wounded need not fear this chapter.

“Doubt that seeks is on its way home. Doubt that hides has already chosen.”
IV

How to Read What God Has Said

Tools you can actually use: context, kind of writing, letting Scripture explain Scripture, and reading alongside the whole church.

“A text pulled from its context becomes a pretext for whatever we already wanted.”
V

Faith Seeking Understanding

The old fight between thinking your way to God and refusing to think at all—and the old answer that ends it. The Spirit who opens our eyes.

“Faith is not the opposite of thinking; it is thinking that has finally found its feet.”
VI

Truth on Its Feet

Truth was never meant only to be known. Walking in it, speaking it in love, and being set free by it.

“Truth you will not live, you do not yet believe.”

A note for those leading a group

Each session stands on its own, but they are meant to be read in order. What gets laid down early is leaned on later. A good rhythm is one session per meeting over six weeks. Read the session before you gather—slowly, and out loud if you can—then open the reflection questions together. The questions are tucked away so the page reads cleanly first. Open them when you are ready to talk.

One request for leaders, and it comes straight out of the third session: guard the honesty of the room. The goal is never to win an argument. The goal is to seek the God who is really there. A group where doubts can be spoken without punishment is a group where truth can actually be found. So let the questions be real.
