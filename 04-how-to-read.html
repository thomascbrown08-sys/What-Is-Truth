Session Four

How to Read What God Has Said

“The Bible is huge, old, and strange—and people make it say opposite things. How do I read it without just finding whatever I already wanted to find?”

That worry is not cynical. It is wise. People have made the Bible say almost anything. Slavery and abolition both quoted it. Every cult has its verses. The honest reader’s fear—what if I just hear my own echo?—is exactly the right fear to have, because it is the start of reading well. The good news is that the Bible is not an inkblot you can read any way you like. It was written to be understood, and there is a craft to reading it faithfully. None of it needs a seminary degree. All of it needs honesty and a little patience.

We start with what the Bible says about itself, because that sets the stakes for everything else:

All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, that the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work.

2 Timothy 3:16–17

“Breathed out by God”—the words themselves carry His authority. And “complete… equipped for every good work”—Scripture is enough. It does not need some other authority propping it up to become trustworthy. It is itself the measure by which we test other claims. That conviction—Scripture as the final, sufficient authority—is the floor we stand on. And it raises the bar on us. If these are God’s own words, then reading them carelessly is no small thing. Paul tells Timothy to be a worker “rightly handling the word of truth” (2 Timothy 2:15)—literally, cutting it straight. There is a crooked way to handle it and a straight way. Here is the straight way, in five plain habits.

Habit oneRead it in context

The most common way to misuse the Bible is to lift one sentence out of its surroundings and make it carry a meaning it never had at home. A verse has neighbors—the sentences before and after, the point of the chapter, the purpose of the whole book. It also has a history: a writer, an audience, an occasion. Meaning lives in those settings. Tear the verse out, and you can make it say almost anything.

A text pulled from its context becomes a pretext for whatever we already wanted.

So the first question is never “What does this mean to me?” It is “What did this mean to them—the one who wrote it, and the ones who first heard it?” Only after we’ve heard it in its own world can we faithfully carry it into ours. The rule is simple to say and humbling to do: read the paragraph, not the fragment; read the book, not the verse.

Habit twoRead for what kind of writing it is

The Bible is a library, not a single book—law and history, poetry and proverb, prophecy and parable, letter and vision. And you don’t read a poem the way you read a contract. When the psalmist says God will cover you “with his pinions” (Psalm 91:4), he is not saying God has feathers. He is painting a picture of shelter. When Jesus tells a parable, He is not reporting the news. He is telling a story that turns to catch you. Reading well means asking first: what kind of writing is this?—and then reading it on its own terms.

In Plain Language

Two old words worth knowing. Exegesis (eks-eh-JEE-sis) means drawing the meaning out of the text—letting it say what it says. Its evil twin, eisegesis (eye-seh-JEE-sis), means reading your own meaning into the text—making it echo you. The whole craft, in one line: get the meaning out; don’t read your meaning in.

Genre just means “kind of writing.” You already do this every day—you don’t panic when someone says “it’s raining cats and dogs.” Bring that same instinct to the page.

Habit threeLet Scripture explain Scripture

Because the whole Bible has one ultimate Author, it does not finally contradict itself—and that gives us a powerful tool: the clear passages explain the unclear ones. When a verse is puzzling, we don’t build a whole teaching on it by itself. We ask what the rest of the Bible, where it speaks plainly, has already settled. The unclear bows to the clear, never the other way around. The Westminster writers stated the rule four centuries ago, and it has not been improved on:

The infallible rule of interpretation of Scripture is the Scripture itself… it must be searched and known by other places that speak more clearly.

Westminster Confession of Faith · I.9

This is also your best guard against the fear we started with—hearing only your own echo. A meaning you can only hold by ignoring everything else the Bible says is almost certainly your echo. A meaning that fits the whole is far more likely the Author’s. Peter warned that some twist the hard parts “to their own destruction” (2 Peter 3:16). Reading the hard parts in light of the plain ones is how we refuse to be among them.

Habit fourObserve, then interpret, then apply

Here is a simple workflow you can use on any passage for the rest of your life. The order matters more than anything. Most misreadings come from racing to the third step before doing the first.

First, observe: What does it actually say? Slow down. Notice the words, the repeats, the joining words (“therefore,” “but,” “because”), the shape of the story or the argument. Most of us think we’ve read a passage when we’ve only skimmed it. Second, interpret: What did it mean—to the writer, to the first hearers, in its context and kind of writing? This is where the first three habits do their work. Only then, apply: What does this ask of me, now? Application is the goal, but it has to grow out of the real meaning, not replace it. We don’t get to skip to “what it means to me” before we’ve asked “what it means.”

This is exactly the pattern of the great public reading in Nehemiah, when God’s people heard the Law after their long exile:

They read from the book, from the Law of God, clearly, and they gave the sense, so that the people understood the reading.

Nehemiah 8:8

Read clearly—then give the sense—so that the people understood. Reading, meaning, understanding: the same straight path. The goal was never just to run our eyes over the words. It was to understand.

Habit fiveRead alongside the whole church

You are not the first person to open this book. For two thousand years, faithful readers—many wiser, more prayerful, and more tested than we are—have wrestled with these same texts and handed down what they found. The great creeds and confessions are not a second Bible. They are the church’s shared memory of what Scripture plainly teaches, written down so each new generation doesn’t have to start from scratch—and so a clever individual can’t quietly reinvent the faith.

Hold this carefully, because the balance is precious. Scripture alone is the final authority. The creeds are true only insofar as they faithfully sum up it; they stand under it, never over it. But reading “just me and my Bible,” cut off from every other reader, is not humility. It is often pride wearing humility’s coat. The honest reader reads with the church across the ages—testing every tradition against the text, yet glad not to be alone. When your fresh, exciting reading of a passage turns out to be one no faithful Christian has ever held in two thousand years, that’s usually not a sign of your insight. It’s usually a sign you’ve wandered.

Deeper Waters

The Reformers taught the perspicuity (clarity) of Scripture: that everything you need for salvation and basic faithful living is plain enough for an ordinary reader to grasp. You don’t need an official interpreter to hand God’s Word down to you. That is a freeing idea—it puts the Bible in your hands. But “clear about the essentials” is not the same as “easy about everything.” Peter admitted some of Paul is “hard to understand” (2 Peter 3:16). So the Reformers held two things together: the main things are clear enough for anyone, and the deep things reward a lifetime of study in the company of the church. Both are true at once. The Bible is shallow enough for a child to wade in and deep enough to drown an elephant—and you are invited into all of it.

The point of it allReading toward a Person

It is possible to master all five habits and still miss everything. A person can become a skilled mechanic of the text and never meet its Author. He can win every argument about the Bible and still walk out of the room, like Pilate, having handled the truth without bowing to it. So we end where the whole study began: the aim of reading the Bible is not mastering a book. It is knowing a Person.

This is why the risen Christ, walking with two heartbroken disciples, opened the whole of Scripture to show them it had been about Him all along: “beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he interpreted to them in all the Scriptures the things concerning himself” (Luke 24:27). Every faithful reading runs, in the end, toward Christ. Augustine gave the final test of whether we’ve understood at all—and it is not a test of cleverness:

Whoever thinks he understands the Scriptures… in such a way that it does not build up the love of God and neighbor, has not yet understood them.

Augustine of Hippo · On Christian Doctrine, I.36

There is the proof that you’ve read rightly: not that you can argue, but that you love God and your neighbor more than when you started. And because the smudged lens from the last session is still on our eyes, we don’t begin with confidence in our own skill. We begin on our knees, with the oldest prayer of every honest reader: “Open my eyes, that I may behold wondrous things out of your law” (Psalm 119:18). Who answers that prayer—and how—is the subject of the next session.

Reflection & Discussiontap to open ›

For personal reflection or small-group conversation. Try practicing one habit together on a real passage before you talk.

  1. Have you ever seen a Bible verse used in a way that ignored its context? What got lost? Can you think of a time you did it yourself?
  2. Of the five habits—context, kind of writing, Scripture explaining Scripture, observe-then-interpret-then-apply, reading with the church—which comes least naturally to you? Why might that be?
  3. We said the order matters: observe, then interpret, then apply—and most misreadings come from rushing to “what it means to me.” Where are you most tempted to skip ahead?
  4. How do you hold together “Scripture alone is the final authority” and “don’t read cut off from the whole church”? Where could each one, by itself, go wrong?
  5. Augustine’s test for understanding Scripture is whether it grows your love of God and neighbor. By that test, how has your recent Bible reading been going—honestly?
  6. Practice: Take Psalm 23 (or any short passage) and walk it through observe–interpret–apply together. What did slowing down at “observe” turn up that you’d normally skip?
