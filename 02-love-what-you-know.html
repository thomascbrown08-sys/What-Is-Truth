Session Two

You Cannot Love What You Will Not Know

“Isn’t sincerity what really counts? Why all this fuss about getting God right—isn’t it enough just to love Him?”

It is a fair question, and it deserves a fair answer. There is a good instinct behind it. God is not impressed by clever theology. He hears the prayers of the simple and the dying. A frightened child’s “help me” reaches heaven just as surely as a scholar’s thick book. All of that is gloriously true, and we will protect it carefully before this session ends.

But the question hides an assumption worth dragging into the light. It assumes that loving God and knowing God are two separate things—so you could do the first while skipping the second. With people, that turns out to be false. Try it with anyone you love. You cannot love a stranger you refuse to learn. You can be infatuated with a picture you’ve invented. But the moment the real person says or does something that doesn’t fit your picture, you find out whether you loved them or only the portrait you painted. Real love wants the real person. It leans in. It asks. It would rather know an uncomfortable truth about the one it loves than hold on to a flattering lie.

You cannot love a God you are unwilling to know.

This is why the Bible will not let worship drift away from truth. In one of His most quoted sentences, Jesus ties the two together so tightly you can’t pull them apart:

God is spirit, and those who worship him must worship in spirit and truth.

John 4:24

Not spirit or truth. Both, fused. Worship that is all feeling and no truth is sincere energy aimed at a target we never bothered to identify—heat with no light. Worship that is all truth and no feeling is correct and cold—light with no heat. God asks for the whole fire. And notice: the “truth” half is not an upgrade for brainy people. It is part of what worship is. To worship in truth is to worship God as He really is, not as we would prefer Him to be.

The mind is not optionalLove God with all your mind

When Jesus is asked for the single greatest commandment—the one sentence that organizes a whole life—He answers with the love of God. And He will not let us love God with only part of ourselves:

And you shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind and with all your strength.

Mark 12:30

With all your mind. The mind is called into the love of God, not let off the hook. This is good news for anyone who has been made to feel that thinking is a threat to devotion—as if the moment you ask a hard question you’ve stepped off holy ground. The opposite is true. Bringing your mind to God is not holding worship back. It is obeying the first commandment more fully. The questioner is not a problem to manage. The questioner, seeking honestly, is doing what Jesus commanded.

So what you believe about God is not a private hobby with no consequences. A.W. Tozer put it with unforgettable weight:

What comes into our minds when we think about God is the most important thing about us.

A. W. Tozer · The Knowledge of the Holy

Most important—because everything else flows from it. A small or false picture of God will shrink your courage, twist your obedience, poison your prayers, and sour your joy. A true and growing knowledge of God will, over time, grow all of them. We don’t study God to win a debate. We study Him because what we think He is like quietly shapes what we become.

The demandGod commands the love of the truth

So far this has all been invitation. But the Bible goes further, and we must not soften it. God does not just recommend the love of truth. He requires it. Turning away from the truth is not a neutral choice in the Bible. It is a moral act, and it carries eternal weight. Paul describes those who perish, and he points to the exact wound:

…because they refused to love the truth and so be saved.

2 Thessalonians 2:10

Read that slowly. Not “they failed a test.” Not “they couldn’t follow the argument.” They refused to love the truth. The problem is the direction of the heart, not the horsepower of the head. There is a way of holding truth at arm’s length that is not ignorance but resistance—and God names it for what it is. Loving the truth is commanded. Pushing it away is condemned. We’ll spend the whole next session on that difference.

But here we have to be exact, because this is the very place where a study like this could wound the people it most wants to help. What God demands is the love of truth and the honesty to seek Him—not scholarship, not sophistication, not a certain reading level. He does not require a degree. He does not require you to solve every hard question before you may come to Him. The thief on the cross knew almost no theology, and he was in paradise that day. The demand is not brains. The demand is that we not turn away from God as He has shown Himself—that we not trade the living God for a comfortable made-up one, and that we love the truth enough to seek it where it can be found.

In Plain Language

God is not handing out a test you can fail by being uneducated. The simplest believer who loves God truly knows Him better than the most learned scholar who studies Him without love. The thing God will not accept is the deliberate turning away—choosing a god we made up because the real One is inconvenient. The demand isn’t “be smart.” The demand is “be honest, and come.”

The idol problemA god who isn’t there

Now we can see why getting God right is not nitpicking. It is the whole game. If truth about God is part of loving God, then a false picture of God is not a slightly-off version of the relationship. It is a relationship with someone who isn’t there. That is the Bible’s real meaning of idolatry, and it is far more unsettling than statues. An idol doesn’t have to be carved. It can be a wrong idea of God held close in the heart: God as a vending machine, God as an easygoing grandfather, God as a cosmic police officer, God as the mascot for my politics. Each one is sincerely worshiped by someone. None of them is there.

Watch how subtle it gets in the first case. At the foot of Mount Sinai, Israel makes the golden calf—and they do not say, “Let’s drop the LORD.” Aaron builds an altar in front of the calf and announces, “Tomorrow shall be a feast to the LORD” (Exodus 32:5). They kept the right name and stuck it on the wrong thing. They were sincere. They were also, in that hour, in a relationship with no one—pouring real devotion into metal. Sincerity did not save them. Sincerity has never been able to make a false god real.

A false god, however sincerely worshiped, saves no one.

Paul names this same swap as the engine under all human ruin. People “exchanged the truth about God for a lie and worshiped and served the creature rather than the Creator” (Romans 1:25). The first sin under all our sins is a swapped picture—trading the real God for a more manageable one. Which is exactly why getting back to God must include getting back the truth about God. You cannot be reconciled to a person you’ve replaced with a portrait.

Deeper Waters

Theologians sometimes tell apart the knowledge of God from knowledge about God. The second can exist without the first—even the demons “believe—and shudder” (James 2:19). But the first never exists without at least some of the second. You cannot know a person you know nothing true about. This is why the Reformers said saving faith has real content (notitia, knowing the facts; and assensus, agreeing they are true) and yet isn’t complete until it becomes fiducia—personal trust, the heart leaning its full weight on Christ. Three Latin words for one living act. Facts alone are not faith. But faith is never less than facts trusted. There is much more here than we can map—but that is the shape of the coastline.

The good news under the demandHe wants to be known

If the session stopped at the demand, it would crush us. But the demand sits inside an amazing piece of good news: the true God has not hidden. He is not a riddle daring us to solve Him or die. He has spoken. He has acted in history. And in the end He came in person—the Word made flesh—so that the invisible God could be seen and known. The God who commands us to love the truth is the same God who has gone to staggering lengths to make the truth findable.

Hear how He describes what He actually delights in:

But let him who boasts boast in this, that he understands and knows me, that I am the LORD who practices steadfast love, justice, and righteousness in the earth. For in these things I delight, declares the LORD.

Jeremiah 9:24

The one thing in all the world worth boasting about is not success or wealth or even cleverness. It is to understand and know Him. And the prophet Hosea turns that delight into an invitation we can take up this week: “Let us know; let us press on to know the LORD” (Hosea 6:3). “Press on” is the language of pursuit—not anxious striving to earn His favor, but the glad, steady movement of a heart toward Someone it has begun to love.

And here is the deepest comfort, the thing that keeps this whole study from turning into mere self-improvement: even our pursuit of Him is His gift before it is our effort. We seek because He first sought us. We press on to know Him because He has already, in Christ and by His Spirit, taken hold of us. “We love because he first loved us” (1 John 4:19)—and we know because He first made Himself known. The demand to love the truth is real. But it lands on people grace has already begun to move. So you can press on without panic. The God you are seeking is the God who put the seeking in you.

Reflection & Discussiontap to open ›

For personal reflection or small-group conversation. Be gentle with one another here—idols are tender things to name.

  1. “You cannot love a God you are unwilling to know.” Think of a time you loved your idea of someone more than the real person. What happened when reality and the picture collided? How might the same thing happen with God?
  2. Jesus commands love with “all your mind.” Have you ever felt that asking hard questions was disloyal to God? Where did that feeling come from—and how does this command answer it?
  3. We drew a hard line: God demands the love of truth and honest seeking, not scholarship. Why does that line matter? Who in your life needs to hear it?
  4. An idol can be a wrong idea of God held close in the heart—the right name on the wrong thing, like at Sinai. Without rushing to the “correct” answer: what false picture of God are you most tempted to prefer because it’s more convenient than the real One?
  5. Jeremiah says the one thing worth boasting in is to “understand and know” God. If that were truly your highest treasure, what would change about an ordinary week?
